Abstract. In response to the limited availability of high-quality Vietnamese medical data, this study presents an exploratory analysis of the ViT5 model fine-tuned on a custom dataset comprising over 12,000 question-answer pairs collected from trusted Vietnamese healthcare platforms, including vinmec.com, tamanhhospital.vn, bookingcare.vn, and edoctor.io. This da-taset spans diverse medical topics, sourced directly from patient inquiries and professional responses, providing a unique resource for Vietnamese-language medical NLP. Our experimental deployment of ViT5 explores its capacity to handle contextually complex medical queries in Vietnamese, evaluating the model’s strengths and limitations in generating coherent, med-ically relevant responses. Findings highlight ViT5’s potential in Vietnamese sequence-to-sequence tasks, though challenges in domain-specific accuracy underscore the need for future work integrating Human-in-the-Loop feed-back. This study establishes a foundational dataset and baseline performance for future Vietnamese medical NLP research.
Keywords: ViT5, Medical Question Answering, Fine-tuning, Healthcare, Vi-etnamese language models.
## 1	Introduction
Vietnam is home to highly skilled healthcare professionals known for their ex-pertise across a wide range of medical fields. Despite this wealth of knowledge, a well-organized, Vietnamese-language medical dataset—especially in a question-and-answer (Q&A) format—is still lacking. This gap poses challenges for pa-tients seeking quick, reliable answers to health-related questions, especially in time-sensitive situations where immediate advice is essential.
In today’s digital era, there is a growing demand for accessible, prompt medi-cal guidance. Patients increasingly look for resources that provide quick re-sponses to their health concerns, enabling them to make informed decisions without waiting for direct consultations. An effective medical Q&A system can play a crucial role in meeting this need, offering timely, reliable advice that can prevent complications or expedite care. 
This study addresses these challenges by exploring the potential of ViT5, a state-of-the-art natural language processing (NLP) model, to build a Vietnamese-language medical Q&A system. Using a large-scale dataset of healthcare Q&A pairs sourced from trusted Vietnamese platforms, we aim to evaluate ViT5’s ca-pability in handling medical queries and highlight the dataset’s contribution as a valuable resource for future Vietnamese NLP research in healthcare.
## 2	Related Work
In recent years, large pretrained language models such as ELMo (Peters et al., 2018), GPT (Brown et al., 2020), and BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) have significant-ly advanced the field of natural language processing (NLP). These models have proven effective in various tasks such as machine translation, text summariza-tion, and question-answering, but their primary development and application have been in English. The Vietnamese NLP landscape has also seen progress, particularly with models like PhoBERT (Nguyen & Tuan, 2020) and BARTpho (Tran et al., 2021), both of which are adapted from BERT and BART, respective-ly. These models have demonstrated strong performance in general tasks like sentiment analysis and text classification.
However, despite these advancements in Vietnamese NLP, there remains a sig-nificant gap in the application of NLP models in the healthcare domain, particu-larly in the context of providing real-time, reliable medical advice. Most of the existing work in Vietnamese healthcare NLP focuses on basic text classification or retrieval-based systems, which are insufficient for complex, context-aware question-answering tasks. For example, chatbots such as the one used on chat.khambenh.gov.vn, which is managed by the Department of Health in Ho Chi Minh City, rely heavily on menu-based interactions and rule-based systems. These systems, while useful for simple queries, are limited in their ability to pro-vide dynamic, personalized medical advice. They lack the flexibility and depth needed to understand complex medical queries and generate accurate, context-specific responses.
The few attempts to incorporate AI into healthcare in Vietnam primarily use structured script-based approaches, which limit the user's ability to ask open-ended questions. This contrasts with state-of-the-art Q&A systems developed globally, such as those using GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) or MedPaLM (Singhal et al., 2022), which are capable of generating responses in natural language and handling a wide range of medical inquiries. These models, however, have not been widely adapted for Vietnamese, and the scarcity of high-quality Vietnamese medical datasets further complicates the development of such systems.
This study seeks to bridge this gap by exploring the viability of ViT5 (Nguyen et al., 2023), an adaptation of T5 (Raffel et al., 2019), for Vietnamese medical Q&A. By fine-tuning ViT5 on a dataset of over 12,000 question-answer pairs from trusted platforms (vinmec.com, tamanhhospital.vn, bookingcare.vn, edoc-tor.io), we aim to evaluate the model’s ability to generate contextually relevant responses for diverse medical inquiries. This work contributes both a new dataset and an exploratory analysis of ViT5’s application in Vietnamese healthcare, providing a foundation for future advancements in this domain.
## 3	Dataset
### 3.1	Data Quality
This study uses a high-quality, Vietnamese-language dataset of over 12,000 ques-tion-answer pairs sourced from four well-known Vietnamese healthcare plat-forms: vinmec.com, tamanhhospital.vn, bookingcare.vn, and edoctor.io. Each platform is recognized for its expert-reviewed medical information, where ques-tions are submitted by patients and answered by licensed healthcare profession-als. This structure ensures that the dataset is both accurate and representative of real-world medical inquiries, covering a wide range of health topics.
To ensure consistency and reliability, the dataset underwent comprehensive preprocessing. Each response was reviewed to maintain uniformity across differ-ent data sources, and irrelevant or incomplete entries were filtered out. By retain-ing only high-quality answers provided by verified doctors, the dataset serves as a trusted resource for training models like ViT5, positioning it as a valuable con-tribution to Vietnamese NLP in healthcare.
### 3.2	Data Collection Methodology

 
Fig. 1. Data Crawling and Processing Pipeline
The data collection process involved customized web scraping techniques tai-lored to each platform's structure
Vinmec: Data was collected by crawling categorized pages containing patient questions and expert answers. Using the requests and BeautifulSoup libraries, URLs were programmatically generated for each medical specialty, and the HTML content was parsed to extract Q&A pairs, which were stored in CSV for-mat for further cleaning and preprocessing.
eDoctor: An API endpoint provided structured JSON data, enabling efficient retrieval of question-answer pairs. Data was iteratively fetched in batches, han-dling pagination to collect over 4,300 records. BookingCare: Data was retrieved through the public API, capturing 700 ques-tion-answer pairs focused on common health inquiries. API responses were stored and filtered to remove duplicates, ensuring only unique and relevant pairs were included in the final dataset.
Each Q&A entry across the datasets was normalized for medical terminology and linguistic consistency, ensuring a cohesive training set. By combining struc-tured API calls and customized web scraping, we were able to gather a diverse, high-quality dataset that underpins this study and contributes to future Vietnam-ese NLP advancements in the healthcare domain.

## 4	Model
In this section, we present the model ViT5 and the fine-tuning process used for developing the Vietnamese medical Q&A system based on it.
### 4.1	ViT5
ViT5 is a Vietnamese adaptation of the popular T5 (Text-to-Text Transfer Trans-former) model, which has shown remarkable performance in various sequence-to-sequence (seq2seq) tasks, such as text generation, translation, and summariza-tion. The T5 model treats all NLP tasks as text-to-text problems, where both in-puts and outputs are sequences of text. This framework makes ViT5 highly suita-ble for generating responses in Q&A systems, especially in the medical domain, where context and language understanding are critical.
ViT5 has been pre-trained on a large corpus of Vietnamese text data, ensuring that it understands the syntactic and semantic nuances of the language. By fine-tuning this pre-trained model on a high-quality dataset of medical Q&A pairs, we aim to leverage its powerful generative capabilities to address the complex que-ries posed by patients.

### 4.2	Experiment: Fine-Tuning ViT5 on Medical Data
In this section, we describe the process of fine-tuning the ViT5 model using the curated dataset of over 12,000 medical question-answer pairs from four major Vietnamese healthcare platforms. 

 
Fig. 2. End-to-End Fine-Tuning Process for ViT5-based Vietnamese Medical Q&A Mod-el
Data Preparation
Dataset Loading and Integration: Data from four sources—Vinmec, Tâm Anh Hospital, BookingCare, and eDoctor—were loaded and concatenated to form a comprehensive dataset. Data distribution among sources was visualized to ensure coverage across diverse topics.
Data Splitting: The integrated dataset was split into training (90%) and test (10%) sets, ensuring the test set contained a representative sample of medical questions.
Tokenization: Using the ViT5 tokenizer from "VietAI/vit5-base" on Hugging Face, each question was prefixed with “hỏi:” and each answer with “trả lời:” to define context. Tokenization was set with a maximum sequence length of 512 tokens for input and 512 for target sequences, truncating or padding as needed.
Custom DatasetLoader Class: A custom PyTorch dataset loader class was de-fined to handle tokenized data, converting token lists into PyTorch tensors for efficient batching and loading during training.
Training Configuration
Learning Rate: An initial learning rate of 5e-5 was used, dynamically adjusted using a linear scheduler for stable training.
Batch Size and Gradient Accumulation: Due to GPU memory limitations, a batch size of 4 was selected, with gradient accumulation over 4 steps. This al-lowed the model to handle large sequence lengths while optimizing memory us-age.
Optimizer: The AdamW optimizer was chosen for its stability with large trans-former models, and weight decay was applied to prevent overfitting.
Early Stopping: Early stopping based on validation loss was employed to avoid overfitting. Training was halted if validation loss did not improve after 3 consecutive epochs.
Training Loop: The model was fine-tuned over a maximum of 10 epochs, with validation loss monitored at each epoch to save the best model. Each epoch con-sisted of forward and backward passes over the training set, with optimizer steps taken every two batches due to gradient accumulation.
Evaluation Metric: The F1 score was used as the primary metric to assess the model’s balance between precision and recall, crucial for medical Q&A tasks.
Implementation and Training: The model and tokenizer were loaded from "VietAI/vit5-base" on Hugging Face. The training script was executed on Kaggle with two T4 GPUs, utilizing CUDA for acceleration.
Model Evaluation and Inference
To evaluate the model, F1 scores were calculated on a sample of test data. Each question in the test set was passed through the model, generating responses via beam search (5 beams) to ensure high-quality outputs. A custom function com-puted F1 scores by comparing generated answers with true answers, providing a quantitative measure of accuracy.
This detailed setup allows future researchers to replicate or build upon this work, potentially integrating improvements such as alternative tokenization strategies, hyperparameter tuning, or domain-specific evaluation metrics to en-hance model effectiveness in Vietnamese medical Q&A applications.
## 5	Results
We evaluated the performance of four ViT5-based models on the medical Q&A dataset using the F1 score, chosen for its balance between precision and recall. This metric is appropriate for assessing relevance and accuracy in generated medical responses, though it may not fully capture the quality of nuanced, con-text-dependent answers typical of healthcare scenarios.

Unlike tasks like classification, where there is a strict right or wrong answer, Q&A tasks—especially in a domain as sensitive as healthcare—require more flexibility in evaluation. Therefore, an F1 score, while useful, might not always reflect the true quality of the response (e.g., the medical advice might be valid, even if phrased differently).
To further evaluate the effectiveness of the models, we tested all four models using a set of 11 general medical questions, covering topics such as emergency symptoms and preliminary diagnosis, counseling on sexually transmitted diseas-es (STDs), digestive symptoms and specialist consultation, mental health and psychological advice, surgery and associated risks, medication use and side ef-fects, vaccination for children, nutrition and lifestyle advice, women’s health and prenatal check-ups, disease prevention advice, and specialist consultation and treatment methods. These questions were designed to represent a broad range of medical topics, including urgent symptoms, preventive care, chronic conditions, and sensitive health issues. The goal was to assess each model’s performance in terms of clarity, relevance, and accuracy when responding to real-world medical inquiries. After a comprehensive evaluation of all four models, model 4 consist-ently provided contextually relevant and medically sound answers across diverse health topics. Its responses effectively addressed urgent symptoms, provided guidance on chronic conditions, and supported preventive care. While the F1 score may understate its capabilities, the qualitative analysis highlights Model 4’s potential in real-world applications. ## 6	Discussion
The fine-tuning process demonstrated that additional training on diverse datasets incrementally improved model performance, as reflected by the F1 scores. How-ever, these improvements are modest, suggesting that the F1 score alone may not fully capture the quality of responses in a healthcare Q&A setting. Medical re-sponses often require nuanced, contextually appropriate answers, where small variations in phrasing could still convey correct advice. The qualitative analysis of the 11-question set further underscores this point, showing that Model 4 was able to generate relevant and contextually aware responses across various medi-cal inquiries, from urgent symptoms to preventive care.
The study highlights the potential and limitations of ViT5 in the healthcare domain, particularly in Vietnamese. While automated metrics provided baseline performance, future research should explore Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) evalua-tion to improve real-world applicability. In HITL, medical professionals would validate model responses and provide feedback, enabling iterative refinement and boosting model reliability. This approach could address some of the com-plexities involved in healthcare Q&A, where relevance and medical accuracy are essential.
## 7	Conclusion
This study presented a foundational effort to adapt ViT5 for Vietnamese medical question-answering, creating a dataset of over 12,000 question-answer pairs and demonstrating the model’s potential for delivering accessible healthcare infor-mation. The results indicate that ViT5, when fine-tuned on diverse medical da-tasets, can produce contextually relevant responses. However, limitations in quantitative metrics like F1 score highlight the need for more nuanced evaluation methods, such as Human-in-the-Loop feedback.
To support further research, we have made our model accessible via Hugging Face at huggingface.co/spaces/tuongvxx1/medBot4_2. Additionally, the dataset and code are available on GitHub at github.com/ChallesVu/VT-VietnameseMedicalQ-ABot-ViT5-SelfCollectedData. We hope these resources will aid in advancing Vietnamese NLP applications in healthcare.

Tuy nhiên, vắc xin có thể gây ra một số tác dụng phụ nghiêm trọng như buồn nôn, nôn, đau bụng, tiêu chảy, mệt mỏi, đau nhức cơ bắp và rối loạn tiêu hóa nên chị không cần quá lo lắng. Tác dụng phụ thì chị nên đưa con đi khám bác sĩ để được tư vấn cụ thể hơn. Chúc chị và bé nhiều sức khỏe.
